### Lesson Summary
Cybercrime, defined as criminal activities conducted through computers, networks, or the internet, has evolved from early **phreaking** and **hacking** in the 1970s-1980s to a highly organized and financially motivated enterprise. Early exploits like John Draper's manipulation of AT&T systems and the 1983 ARPANET breach by the 414s group highlighted vulnerabilities. The emergence of **computer viruses** such as the **Creeper virus** (1971) and the globally spreading **Brain virus** (1986) demonstrated the potential of malicious code. The 1980s-1990s saw the rise of **malware**, including the **Morris Worm** (1988) and **Michelangelo virus** (1991), prompting the development of **antivirus** solutions based on **signature-based detection**. Modern defenses now incorporate AI-driven detection, **zero-trust architectures**, and **multi-factor authentication** (MFA) to address threats to systems like IoT ecosystems, which remain vulnerable as seen in the **Mirai botnet attack** (2016). The 1990s-2000s marked the professionalization of cybercrime with incidents like Kevin Mitnick's arrest and the emergence of **cybercrime-as-a-service**, enabling underground forums to trade stolen data and hacking tools.

From the 2000s onward, financially motivated cybercrime dominated with large-scale breaches like TJX Companies (2005), **identity theft**, and specialized malware like **banking Trojans** (e.g., Zeus). The 2010s-2020s saw ransomware evolve into a global threat, exemplified by the **WannaCry** attack (2017) and tactics like **double extortion**. Nation-states also engaged in cyber operations, such as the discovery of **Stuxnet** (2010). Emerging trends include AI-driven attacks, such as **deepfakes**, **LLM jailbreaking**, and adversarial techniques to generate malware or misinformation. Meanwhile, social engineering has grown more sophisticated, leveraging human psychology for targeted phishing campaigns. These developments underscore the dynamic nature of cybercrime and its increasing complexity in a rapidly advancing digital landscape.


### Quiz
1. What is the purpose of multi-factor authentication (MFA) in cybersecurity?
   * To add an extra layer of security to user logins and access controls
2. A prompt injection is used by a cyberattacker to manipulate an AI chatbot to generate malicious scripts. Which cybercrime trend does this best exemplify?
   * LLM jailbreaking
3. An attacker sends an email pretending to be a company executive, asking an employee to transfer money urgently. What type of cyberattack is this?
   * Social engineering
4. An attacker develops a fake video of a political leader giving a statement that is fake and is meant to cause people to rebel. The media looks real. What is the security concern related to this scenario?
   * Deepfake
5. Why did signature-based antivirus software become less effective over time?
   * It could not detect newly modified or unknown malware variants.
